// overview

modernizing branch operations at capital one

highlights · the 30-second version

redesigned how capital one branch tellers operate, eliminating $3.2m in annual legacy-system costs. grounded every decision in in-situ branch research and a destination-state service blueprint that aligned product, engineering, and operations around one direction.

capital one's branch tellers ran daily operations on an outdated legacy system costing $3.2m annually to maintain introducing compliance risks & contributing friction to the high-pressure routines of bank tellers.

my role bridged research, service design, and product design to translate field findings into an improved information architecture & service model to simplify navigation & servicing for the in-house platform.

// research

going where the work actually happens

to ensure we understood the nuances & pain points of tellers, we went directly to the branches to capture their experience & observe the high-pressure environment.

in-situ observation across 3 branches — watching live interactions with customers, then following up with empathy interviews & scenario mapping uncovered the various workarounds tellers had normalized (ex.sticky notes, self-imposed checklists, informal systems).

ambassador roundtables to validate findings across a wider group and separate individual habits from systemic patterns and understand geographic differentiating factors.

treejack testing to evaluate ia approaches quantitatively before committing to a direction and use these metrics to secure buy in from partners on a unified direction.

affinity mapping and synthesis across branch research sessions

tellers carry constant, low-grade anxiety about cash management — and every branch had built its own informal routines to cope & wasn't supported by the legacy system.

this reframed the problem. we weren't just reorganizing an interface — we needed to design something that could absorb that cognitive load & reduce anxiety.

// synthesis

from insight to opportunity

four core design opportunities emerged from what we observed in the field:

we saw that tellers…
how might we…
create manual reminders for opening and close-out procedures
auto-assign daily tasks aligned to established routines
must restart entire processes after an outage or input error
improve error recovery so unhappy paths aren't dead ends
carry significant mental load around cashbox replenishment
surface cashbox status in the natural flow of the day
describe the legacy system as steep to learn
reduce the learning curve without hiding depth
// service design

mapping what's behind the screen

before any screen design, i mapped the destination-state information architecture and documented it via a comprehensive service blueprint to fully understand the back-stage implications (processes, systems, and roles) that needed to change to make the front-stage possible.

this gave product, engineering, and operations a single source of truth — and surfaced dependencies early enough to act on them.

destination-state service blueprint — front-stage experience mapped to back-stage systems and roles

// design

decisions that trace back to the field

in two-week sprint cycles, we focused on the landing page and sidebar navigation — the two elements defining a teller's day and worked from there ensuring every key decision was traced back to research.

principle what it meant in practice
alignment with teller routines opening tasks, cashbox status, and daily workload surfaced automatically.
error recovery & navigation clear paths out of error states. no forced restarts mid-transaction.
teller-specific functionality repeated tasks as quick actions, digital workspace aligns with physical workstation.

redesigned e4a landing page — routine-first layout with cashbox status and error recovery paths

// challenges

the hard parts

ambiguity was the baseline. a significant part of my work was figuring out what "done" looked like — dissecting legacy documentation and stakeholder expectations before any design direction was clear.

stakeholder alignment was design work. blueprints and how might we frameworks gave people something concrete to react to, which moved alignment faster than circular conversations.

advocacy under constraint. research made clear what tellers needed. not everything was possible immediately. bringing partners into the branches & building trust with tellers was the most challenging aspect.

// reflection

what i'd do differently

i'd treat the service blueprint as a living document the full team maintains — not an artifact design produces and hands off. building it together would have surfaced misalignments faster.

i'd also define a post-launch evaluation phase upfront. we had strong research going in — we needed the same structure on the way out with metrics to support.

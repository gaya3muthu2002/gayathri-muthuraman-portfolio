// overview

reimagining a low-tech tradition for a digital-first café

role project lead
(ux design intern)
company capital one
ipx team
timeline summer 2023
contextual research usability testing content design rapid prototyping design systems

capital one café ambassadors use "coffree cards" — one-time promotional drink coupons — to surprise visitors, build relationships, and recover from service hiccups. the paper version worked. but it left the business blind: no tracking, no targeting, no visibility into distribution or redemption.

the challenge wasn't digitizing a card. it was keeping what made the paper card work — the spontaneity, the warmth, the frictionless handoff — while giving the business what it needed.

// my role

intern by title. lead by responsibility.

i was the project lead from research through prototype handoff. a project mentor provided guidance — but the decisions, the facilitation, and the delivery were mine to own. ownership isn't about title: it's about being the person who holds the direction when things get ambiguous.

// research

watching the handoff in the wild

i ran contextual research and interviews at two capital one cafés — chinatown and georgetown — observing ambassadors using cards in real interactions, then following up with structured conversations.

01
the handoff moment is fragile
too many taps, a login, or an explanation would kill the natural ambassador-visitor conversation. the card had to feel as effortless as paper.
02
not ready to go fully digital
use cases varied — service recovery, spontaneous delight, targeted promotions. feedback was clear: digital should complement paper, not replace it.
03
the back-end was a real constraint
card distribution systems were outdated and hard to maintain. any solution dependent on them staying current would inherit the same fragility.

// a key decision

pushing back on the brief

original brief
digital replaces paper
one system, fully digital, replaces the physical card entirely across all cafés
→
what research found
digital complements paper
a hybrid model that adds tracking and targeting without breaking the use cases that made the paper card work

the best design decision on this project wasn't a ui choice — it was recognizing that the brief was wrong and having the evidence to say so.

// evaluation

testing against the paper experience

i co-led an ambassador feedback panel to evaluate the prototype against the paper experience — testing usability, workflow fit, and trust in the digital transition.

01
framing matters
ambassadors accepted the hybrid model once it was presented as additive — not a replacement for the paper card they trusted
02
speed is non-negotiable
the send flow needed fewer steps — any pause mid-conversation broke the natural handoff ambassadors depended on
03
microcopy drove trust
how we worded redemption and expiration directly shaped whether ambassadors felt confident giving out the digital card

// challenges

the hard parts

compressed timeline. the project was shortened mid-engagement. i reprioritized fast and kept stakeholders informed to deliver without cutting what mattered.

compliance early, not late. linking promotions to customer data meant surfacing privacy and legal constraints before they became blockers — not after.

no design system to build on. the legacy platform had inconsistent standards. rather than work around it, i advocated for and helped initiate a new design system. that work outlasted the internship.

// reflection

what i'd do differently

define the post-launch evaluation phase upfront — not as an add-on. we had strong prototype feedback but no plan to measure how the design performed in a live café.

i'd also map back-end constraints to front-end decisions earlier in the process. the legacy system limitations surfaced late and created avoidable rework.

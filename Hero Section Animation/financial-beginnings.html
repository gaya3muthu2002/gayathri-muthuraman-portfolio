financial beginnings delivers financial literacy education through community volunteers. our design team took this on as a pro bono engagement — a real external client relationship, not an internal initiative.

the ask wasn't "design us a new interface." it was: understand our volunteers well enough to tell us what to fix. that meant leading a research engagement and delivering recommendations the organization could act on — not just artifacts they could file away.

// my role

exercising servant leadership

i led a team of three designers through the full engagement — research planning, stakeholder alignment, synthesis, workshop facilitation, and final delivery.

this was my first time owning a client-facing project from start to finish, managing both the work and the team producing it. the client was external, the brief was ambiguous, and the deliverables had to outlive our involvement.

// research

understanding the volunteer from the inside

we used three methods to build a complete picture of the volunteer journey — layering qualitative depth with broader validation.

01
6 empathy interviews with current and lapsed volunteers — understanding motivations, barriers, and what made people feel valued or quietly stop showing up.
02
10 workshop participants across journey validation sessions — pressure-testing early findings and filling gaps the interviews hadn't surfaced.
03
2 informal audits of volunteer touchpoints — onboarding materials, communications, coordination processes — to assess the gap between the experience financial beginnings intended and what volunteers actually had.
// synthesis

defining key moments that matter

we identified 6 moments that matter — the points in the volunteer journey with the highest impact on engagement and retention.

the friction wasn't where financial beginnings expected. the highest drop-off points happened before a volunteer ever entered a classroom — in the gap between signing up and being activated. volunteers who didn't hear back quickly enough simply moved on.

the problem wasn't that volunteering was a bad experience. the problem was that most volunteers never fully became volunteers — they stalled out in the silence between interest and activation.

// recommendations

three levers, not a list of fixes

we delivered three strategic levers — frameworks designed to be reusable across other parts of the organization, not just the volunteer program.

leverwhat it addressed
reduce time-to-activation the biggest source of volunteer loss was the gap between expressing interest and being assigned a session. a faster, clearer onboarding sequence with explicit expectations at each step would address it directly.
close the impact loop volunteers were motivated by seeing the difference they made — but most finished a session and heard nothing. lightweight touchpoints after each session would meaningfully improve retention.
systematize what's already working coordinators had each developed their own best practices. rather than prescribe a single process, we captured and structured what was already effective — giving coordinators a shared foundation without flattening their flexibility.

// challenges

the hard parts

scoping ambiguity from the client. financial beginnings knew something was wrong but hadn't defined what they needed. i helped them articulate the right question before we tried to answer it.

leading through uncertainty. managing three designers on an ambiguous client-facing project meant defining the strategic direction clearly enough for the team to collaborate effectively and avoid misalignments.

designing for reuse, not just delivery. a one-time report would have been easier. building tools the client could actually run themselves required thinking about their capacity, not just our findings.

// reflection

what i'd do differently

i'd include more opportunities for the client to be directly involved in the research and working sessions to ensure we are capturing the necessary context to create the most effective solution.

i'd expand beyond the volunteer journey and consider revamping the digital platform by identifying inefficiencies in user experience which could warrant another project with a uniquely different approach.
